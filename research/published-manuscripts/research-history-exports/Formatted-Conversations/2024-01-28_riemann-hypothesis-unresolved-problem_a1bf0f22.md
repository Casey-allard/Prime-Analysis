**Solution By Steps**

*Step 1: Understanding the Riemann Hypothesis*
The Riemann Hypothesis, proposed by Bernhard Riemann in 1859, is a conjecture about the distribution of the zeros of the Riemann zeta function, ζ(s), which is defined for complex numbers s. The hypothesis states that all non-trivial zeros of the zeta function have a real part equal to 1/2. 

*Step 2: Current Status of the Hypothesis*
As of my last update in April 2023, the Riemann Hypothesis remains an unsolved problem in mathematics. It is one of the seven Millennium Prize Problems for which the Clay Mathematics Institute has offered a prize of $1,000,000 for a correct proof or counterexample.

*Step 3: Implications of the Hypothesis*
The importance of the Riemann Hypothesis lies in its deep connection with number theory, particularly the distribution of prime numbers. Its resolution would have far-reaching implications in number theory and other areas of mathematics.

**Final Answer**
As of now, there is no known proof or disproof of the Riemann Hypothesis. It remains one of the most famous and long-standing unsolved problems in mathematics.

**Key Concept**
The Riemann Hypothesis and its relation to the zeros of the Riemann zeta function.

**Key Concept Explanation**
The Riemann zeta function, ζ(s), initially defined for complex numbers s with a real part greater than 1, can be extended to other values of s (except for s=1 where it has a simple pole) through analytic continuation. The function plays a key role in number theory due to its relation to the distribution of prime numbers, which is formalized in the Euler product formula. The Riemann Hypothesis conjectures that all non-trivial zeros of this function (zeros that are not negative even integers) lie on the "critical line" in the complex plane, where the real part of s is 1/2. The locations of these zeros are believed to be deeply connected to the distribution of prime numbers, and proving this hypothesis would significantly enhance our understanding of prime numbers and numerous other areas in mathematics.
