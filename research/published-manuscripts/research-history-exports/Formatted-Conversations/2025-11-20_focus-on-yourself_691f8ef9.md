It's understandable that you're exhausted. Being caught in a repeated emotional cycle—especially one involving intense reactions, separation, and reconciliation—can burn a person out, no matter how much compassion they have.

A few things can be true simultaneously:

### **1. She may genuinely be experiencing abandonment panic.**
People with BPD often feel *real* emotional distress when communication stops suddenly, even if they triggered the conflict. That emotional pain is valid *from her perspective.*

### **2. *You* can also be genuinely overwhelmed and need space to regulate.**
Stepping back to protect your mental health doesn’t make you selfish. It makes you human. Relationships—*especially high-intensity ones*—require emotional capacity, and you were at zero when she hung up on you. It’s reasonable to protect your energy before re-engaging.

### **3. The pattern itself is the real problem—not this specific instance.**
If you're recognizing a cyclical loop like:

**Stress → Trigger → Outburst → Withdrawal → Panic → Reconciliation → Repeat**

…then taking space isn't just a reaction—it's a sign you're aware the dynamic is unsustainable in its current form.

That’s not about blame. It's about the **system between you two**, not just the individuals.

### **4. It's not healthy for her if the relationship depends on instant responses.**
If you feel *obligated* to respond immediately to prevent emotional collapse, that isn’t connection—that's emotional dependency, and it puts you in the role of regulator rather than partner.

Even if you love her, that dynamic will destroy you both long-term unless boundaries and emotional regulation skills are in place (ideally with support from her therapist, DBT, etc.).

Not from what you described.
