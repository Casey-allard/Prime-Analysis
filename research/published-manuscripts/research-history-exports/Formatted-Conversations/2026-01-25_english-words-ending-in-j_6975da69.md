What is an English based word that ends in the letter J? Is there one that does originate from another language?

### Assistant (2026-01-25 03:55:46)

Short answer: **native English words ending in the letter *J*** basically do **not** exist. English phonotactics hate final **J** the way cats hate water.

Longer, less romantic answer:

## Native English
- **None.**  
  English words do not naturally end in the letter **J**.  
  Historically, **J** is treated as a consonant that *demands* a vowel after it. When English inherited words that *sound* like they might end in a J sound, they usually get:
  - **-dge** (judge, bridge)
  - **-ge** (rage, age)
  - or a silent helper vowel slapped on out of orthographic anxiety.

## Loanwords that *do* end in J
Yes, English cheats by importing words from languages that are fine ending things with **J**.

### Legitimate examples used in English:
- **raj**  
  - From **Hindi / Sanskrit**  
  - Meaning: rule, reign, or sovereignty (especially British rule in India)  
  - Widely accepted in English dictionaries  
  - Ends cleanly in **J**, no vowel chaperone

- **haj**  
  - Variant spelling of **hajj**  
  - From **Arabic**  
  - Refers to the Islamic pilgrimage to Mecca  
  - Less common than *hajj*, but attested

These survive because English treats them as **foreign lexical items** rather than fully domesticated citizens.

## Why this happens
English spelling rules evolved under:
- Latin and French influence
- A deep discomfort with final voiced affricates
- The need to visually signal pronunciation using extra letters

So instead of letting **J** stand alone at the end like a grown adult, English insists on padding it.

## Bottom line
- **English-origin word ending in J:** ❌ No  
- **English-accepted loanword ending in J:** ✅ Yes  
- **Most solid example:** **raj**

Language is a messy inheritance system full of arbitrary rules, and this is one of them.
