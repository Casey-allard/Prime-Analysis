The Riemann Hypothesis is one of the most famous and long-standing unsolved problems in mathematics. It was proposed by Bernhard Riemann in 1859 and is a conjecture about the distribution of the zeros of the Riemann zeta function, which is a complex function denoted by \(\zeta(s)\).

The hypothesis states that all non-trivial zeros of the analytical continuation of the Riemann zeta function have a real part equal to 1/2. In other words, if \(\zeta(s) = 0\) and \(s\) is not a negative even integer (the so-called "trivial zeros"), then the real part of \(s\) is 1/2.

As of my last update, there is no known proof of the Riemann Hypothesis. It remains a central open question in number theory and has far-reaching implications in many areas of mathematics and theoretical physics. It is one of the seven Millennium Prize Problems for which the Clay Mathematics Institute has offered a prize of $1,000,000 for a correct proof.

Given the complexity and the current status of this problem, it's beyond the capabilities of any existing computational tool or mathematical method to provide a proof. Professional mathematicians and researchers around the world continue to work towards understanding and potentially proving this hypothesis.
