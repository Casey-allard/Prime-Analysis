**Green-Tao Theorem**

The Green-Tao theorem is a landmark result in number theory, discovered by Ben Green and Terence Tao in 2004. This theorem states that there are arbitrarily long arithmetic progressions of prime numbers. In other words, for any natural number \( k \), there exists an arithmetic progression of prime numbers containing exactly \( k \) terms. This result is a significant breakthrough because it confirms that primes, despite being distributed seemingly at random among the natural numbers, contain patterns that can be mathematically defined and proven.

**Solution By Steps**

*Step 1: Understanding Arithmetic Progressions*

An arithmetic progression (AP) is a sequence of numbers in which each term after the first is obtained by adding a constant, called the common difference, to the preceding term. For example, the sequence 3, 7, 11, 15, ... is an arithmetic progression with a common difference of 4.

*Step 2: Recognizing the Significance of the Theorem*

Before the Green-Tao theorem, it was conjectured but not proven that such sequences exist within the prime numbers. Primes are numbers greater than 1 that have no divisors other than 1 and themselves. The theorem's proof shows that the primes, although they become less frequent as numbers get larger, still form infinitely many long sequences where the difference between consecutive numbers in the sequence is constant.

*Step 3: The Methodology Behind the Proof*

Green and Tao used methods from additive combinatorics, a field that studies additive properties of numbers, to demonstrate their theorem. They built upon earlier work, including Szemerédi's theorem on arithmetic progressions in dense subsets of the integers, and introduced new techniques to deal with the distribution of prime numbers.

**Final Answer**

The Green-Tao theorem asserts that for any given positive integer \( k \), there exists an arithmetic progression consisting entirely of prime numbers, and this progression contains exactly \( k \) terms. This means that no matter how large \( k \) is, you can always find a sequence of \( k \) prime numbers where the difference between any two consecutive numbers is the same.

Arithmetic Progressions in Prime Numbers

Wiles's approach began with the realization that Fermat's Last Theorem could be connected to the properties of certain elliptic curves. *Step 2: The Modularity Theorem*

The Modularity Theorem posits that every elliptic curve over the rational numbers is associated with a modular form.
