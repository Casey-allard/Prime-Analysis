## the numbers π, φ and e

The last of the three numbers, 【106†`e`†en.wikipedia.org】 (2.71828182...) is Euler's number and also known as the base of the natural logarithm. It, too, can be defined geometrically—it is the unique real number, `e`, for which the function `f(x) = e^x` has a tangent of slope 1 at `x=0`. Like `\pi`, `e` appears throughout mathematics. For example, `e` is central in the expression for the 【107†normal distribution†en.wikipedia.org】 as well as the definition of 【108†entropy†en.wikipedia.org】. And if you've ever heard of someone talking about 【109†log plots†en.wikipedia.org】 ... well, there's `e` again! 

Two of these numbers can be seen together in mathematics' 【110†most beautiful equation†en.wikipedia.org】, the Euler identity: `e^{i\pi} = -1`. The tau-oists would argue that this is even prettier: `e^{i\tau} = 1`.
