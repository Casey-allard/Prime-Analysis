The Riemann Hypothesis, one of the most famous and long-standing unsolved problems in mathematics, posits that all non-trivial zeros of the Riemann zeta function have their real parts equal to 1/2. This hypothesis is a central part of the field of number theory and has deep implications for the distribution of prime numbers.

As of my last update in April 2023, there is no known proof for the Riemann Hypothesis. It has withstood the efforts of many brilliant mathematicians since it was first proposed by Bernhard Riemann in 1859. The hypothesis is one of the seven Millennium Prize Problems for which the Clay Mathematics Institute offers a prize of one million dollars for a correct proof or counter-example.

The challenge in proving the Riemann Hypothesis lies in its deep connection to the distribution of prime numbers, which are fundamental but still not fully understood in mathematics. The hypothesis has connections to various areas of mathematics, including analytic number theory, complex analysis, and spectral theory.

Any claim of a proof would need to undergo rigorous peer review and validation by the mathematical community, a process that can take a considerable amount of time. As such, if a proof or disproof has been found very recently, it would still be under scrutiny and not yet confirmed as valid.
