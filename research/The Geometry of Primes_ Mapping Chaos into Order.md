1. **Step 1: Finding the Gap:**  Isolate the "prime gap" ( $g$ ), the distance between a prime and its predecessor. This gap represents the isolated empty space that will define our radial metric.  
2. **Step 2: Calculating the Reciprocal Radius:**  The radial distance ( $r$ ) from the origin is defined as the reciprocal of the gap ( $r \= 1/g$ ). This creates a "centripetal compression" effect: larger gaps yield smaller radii. For example, Prime 5 (gap of 2\) yields a radius of 0.5, while the transition between 13 and 17 (gap of 4\) yields a smaller radius of 0.25, yanking the point closer to the center.

This prevents the formation of predictable spokes or clusters, scattering the points in a way that allows the underlying global order to emerge without geometric interference.Through these three rules, the erratic jumps of the number line are transformed into an  **isometry** —a stable, two-dimensional coordinate on a polar grid.
